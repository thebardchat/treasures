## Lyrics

And then he was gripping a steering wheel.

Not the truck. Not the cupholder rattle. Not the forty-seventh mile. A different steering wheel. Smaller. Worn at ten and two from hands that had gripped it for years, for routes, for fares, for the long drives through countries that weren't his telling stories to strangers who weren't strangers yet but would be by the time the smoke trailed away.

The radio was playing something beautiful.

Happy. The kind of music that doesn't ask you to feel something — it just feels something near you and you catch it the way you catch warmth from a fire you didn't build. Strings. A horn. Something Mediterranean. Something that sounded like a kitchen where a man cooked for a woman and said *the food is not wearing a red dress* and the laugh lived in the corner for weeks.

He was driving.

The last client was stepping out. Grateful the way clients are grateful — not for the ride, for the conversation. The story. The stories. The man behind the wheel who turned a fare into a friendship in the time it took to cross the city. The client closed the door. The smoke trailed. The car kept running because the car always kept running. The car didn't know how to stop.

---

He looked at his hands.

These hands.

He knew these hands.

Dark. Strong. The knuckles that ached differently — the specific ache of a man who has gripped a steering wheel across borders and time zones and versions of himself that started as temporary and became permanent and became the whole life. The photograph on the dash. The woman. The two children. The yard with the wall.

He was Pepe.

---

The phone rang.

Not a banana. A real phone. The phone that sat in the center console between the gearshift and the tin of mints and the receipts from fares he'd never file. The phone that connected him to the next ride, the next story, the next stranger who would become a friend in the time it took to cross the city.

He answered it.

*"This is Pepe — I'm the pep in your step! Where can I pick you up?"*

The voice came out of him like a song he'd been singing his whole life. Bright. Warm. The greeting of a man who has decided that every fare begins with joy because the alternative is silence and silence in a cab is a country he refuses to live in.

The voice on the other end said a name.

---

The feeling in his stomach dropped.

Not the slow drop — the trapdoor. The floor-gone-from-under-you drop. The drop that starts in the gut and doesn't stop at the gut, it keeps going, down through the legs, down through the pedals, down through the rubber and the road and the earth beneath the road into some basement of the body where the things you already know but haven't confirmed yet are stored in boxes you've been stepping around for months.

He said: *"Who is it?"*

He knew the name. He knew the name very well. He knew the name the way you know the name of the doctor who is going to tell you the thing the body already told you three months ago but the mind needs a man in a coat to make it real.

The voice said the name again.

And finished with: *"The Detective."*

---

The world stood still.

Not the stutter. Not the skip. Not the groove going shallow. The world — the actual, physical, turning world with its taxes and its Tuesdays and its trains — stood still. Time had no relevance at all. He had no relevance at all. You had no relevance at all.

He was speaking to himself.

The detective on the phone was the man he had hired. Three weeks ago. In a bar that could have been any bar because all bars where desperate men hire other men to confirm the thing they already know look the same — dim, sticky, the kind of light that forgives the conversation but not the reason for it.

He had hired a detective.

To follow his wife.

Because he knew. He had always known. The way the old man at the gas station knew — the way you know a thing you put down in a parking lot in 1987 and the parking lot kept it and you kept going. Pepe knew. The lipstick that appeared and disappeared. The red dress worn to places he wasn't invited. The twelve minutes she thought he didn't know about. The side door. The shoes by the exit.

He knew.

He just needed someone else to see it so the seeing was real and the real was not something he had invented in the long hours behind the wheel where the stories ran out and the silence moved in and the silence asked questions that the stories couldn't cover.

---

*"Do you have anything yet, Detective?"*

His voice was steady. The voice of a man who has driven through weather systems and countries and the specific geography of a marriage that was eroding from the inside the way buildings erode from the pipes outward. Steady. The steadiness was a performance. The steadiness was the last thing the man behind the wheel had control over and he held it the way the banana held the knowing — because it had been held, because the holding was all that was left.

The detective said: *"I have her dead in my sights. She just met a man at the diner. I am taking pictures like you asked. Would you like to meet me around the corner so you do not have to see this in person?"*

---

The detective was at a table.

The detective had been at a table the whole time.

Not floating. Not above. Not in the crane shot. Not in the out-of-body. At a table. In the diner. With a coffee that went cold and a camera that didn't and the professional, clinical efficiency of a man who watches other people's worst moments for a living and files them under the name on the invoice.

The detective was sitting in the same diner as the woman in the red dress. As the Englishman with the flowers. As the beggar and the waiter and the waitress and the bartender and the solitary man and the loud man and his four brothers.

He was one of the seven.

He had always been one of the seven.

The eighth figure. The one nobody counted. The man at the table with the cold coffee and the camera and the case file that had a name on it this time — a real name, a client's name, a name that sounded like joy and meant heartbreak.

Pepe.

---

*"Yes,"* Pepe said. *"Meet me at the coffee shop two blocks down. I'm twenty minutes."*

Twenty minutes.

He said twenty minutes the way you say twenty minutes when you know the twenty minutes is a lie and the lie is the last kindness you can offer yourself before the proof arrives and the proof turns the knowing into the known and the known is a country you can never leave.

---

He looked up toward the diner.

And the beautiful red dress he bought her stood out.

Red. The kind of red that knows what it's doing. The kind that sits in a room and doesn't announce itself until someone looks directly at it and then it's all they see. He had bought that dress. He had handed her the bag from the shop she didn't recognize. He had said *the food is not wearing a red dress* and she had laughed and the laugh had lived in the kitchen corner and the kitchen corner was the last place in the house where the marriage still worked.

The red dress was a bull's eye.

And he was the bull.

Headed to take out the matador.

---

At some point he let go of the steering wheel.

He didn't remember deciding to let go. The hands — those hands, the ones that had gripped the wheel across borders, the ones that ached at the knuckles, the ones that the detective had felt in the inhabitation and thought belonged to a cab driver in another country — those hands opened. The fingers extended. The wheel turned on its own.

The car found the diner.

The smoke trailed.

---

The first thud was the curb.

The second thud was softer. Something that gave. Something at the edge of the sidewalk that had been standing where things stand when they are not seen and are not expected to move. The beggar. The man with the food in his hands. The man with the family in the alley. The man who welcomed the peace.

The thuds became more relaxing the more it happened.

That's the thing nobody tells you about the end. It doesn't get worse. It gets quieter. Each impact was softer than the last. Each collision was a sound that was also a silence. The curb. The sidewalk. The tables. The terrace. The brick that had been glowing in the European light. Each one a note in a song that was winding down, that was finding its final tempo, that was slowing the way records slow when someone lifts the arm and the needle drags and the music stretches into something deeper and slower and more honest than the song was ever meant to be.

---

The car hit the bar.

The window — the front window, the one the headlights had passed through in every track without anyone noticing — the window became something the car went through. Glass and brick and light and the specific sound of a room that had been holding seven lives suddenly holding an eighth.

His body went through.

And the last thing — the very last thing — was the bottle.

---

Spinning.

In the air. Behind the back. Over the shoulder. The amber glass catching the light from the overhead rack — the same light, the same arc, the same ten-thousand-times gesture. The bartender's hands had released the bottle one second before the car came through the window. The bottle was already in the air. The bottle was always in the air. The bottle didn't know about the car. The bottle didn't know about Pepe. The bottle didn't know about the red dress or the detective or the twenty minutes or the coffee shop two blocks down.

The bottle was doing what bottles do when The bartender's hands release them.

It was flying.

It was the bartender's last act.

The top-shelf glass hit Pepe in the head with the perfect accuracy of a man who had never poured amateur and would not pour amateur now, not even now, not even at the end of the show, not even as the car came through his stage and the rubber mat buckled and the marble cracked and the four-by-twelve-foot world collapsed into something that was no longer a world but was still, somehow, a pour.

---

Silence.

The kind that doesn't come after noise. The kind that was always there underneath the noise, waiting for the noise to finish its business and leave.

---

He ended where he began.

The diner. The table. The cold coffee. The camera. The case file.

Except the outcome never changed.

That split second — the one second, the same second, the second that held everything and nothing and the proposal and the betrayal and the beggar and the waitress and the bartender and the solitary man and the loud man and the dream and the banana phone and the planet filing its taxes — that second still happened just the same.

Every version of the second happened.

The one where Pepe drove through the window. The one where Pepe's car passed slowly through the crowd. The one where the detective ran and spun Pepe around and took him to the pub. The one where the headlights went through everyone without touching. The one where the beggar welcomed the peace. The one where the beggar stood at the edge. The one where the bartender's bottle was the last act and the one where the bartender's bottle was just another flip.

All of them happened.

All of them didn't happen.

The second holds both.

The second has always held both.

---

He got up from his table.

The detective. The real one. The one who had been sitting there the whole time with the cold coffee and the camera and the file with Pepe's name on it. The one who had watched the woman in the red dress sit down. The one who had watched the Englishman lean forward. The one who had called Pepe and said *I have her dead in my sights* and then offered to meet at the coffee shop because the detective understood — the way all detectives understand — that seeing the proof is different from hearing the proof and the difference is the distance between a marriage and a crime scene.

He got up.

The loud sounds were gone.

The mumbles of all those people — the waiter serving, the waitress arguing, the bartender shaking, the couple reaching, the loud man hugging, the beggar asking, the solitary man turning pages — all of it had ceased to exist. Not ended. Ceased. The way a radio ceases when you turn it off — the signal is still out there, the broadcast is still happening, the station is still playing the song, but the receiver has been turned off and the receiver was the detective and the detective was done receiving.

He placed the fedora upon his head.

And walked away.

---

The case was solved.

Not closed — solved. There is a difference. Closed means the file goes back in the drawer. Solved means the answer was found and the answer was true and the truth has a weight and the weight is now his to carry and he carries it out the door and into the street and the street is quiet and the diner is behind him and the smoke has settled and the glass has settled and somewhere in there — somewhere in the wreckage or the peace or the version of the second where none of it happened — Pepe is still driving. Still telling stories. Still saying *I'm the pep in your step*. Still cooking for a woman in a red dress who is still laughing in a kitchen corner where the laugh still lives.

The detective walked.

The fedora cast a shadow.

The street was empty.

The record stopped.

---

Not the needle lifting — the record. The vinyl. The actual disc. The thing that had been spinning since Track 000, since the groove, since the needle dropped and the dust sang and the album breathed its first breath. The record stopped.

Not because it was over.

Because the detective walked away from the turntable.

Some albums end when the music stops.

This one ended when the listener left the room.

## Liner Notes
Track 014 is the twist that reframes the entire album. Pepe hired the detective. The detective was working for Pepe the whole time — sitting at a table in the diner, taking pictures, watching the woman in the red dress. The detective was the eighth figure in the diner. The one nobody counted. The cold coffee wasn't atmosphere — it was a stakeout. The "out-of-body" experiences, the inhabitations, the substrate firing — all of it was the detective's mind processing the room while he sat at his table doing his job. Every track on Side B was the detective's empathy running at substrate level while he professionally, clinically watched his client's wife sit across from another man in a dress the client bought her. Then the phone call. Pepe — "I'm the pep in your step!" — calling the detective who calls him back with the confirmation. The voice that had told stories through pubs that shook with songs now hearing: "I have her dead in my sights." Twenty minutes. Pepe says twenty minutes. Pepe lets go of the steering wheel. The car finds the diner. The thuds get quieter, not louder. The beggar at the edge of the sidewalk — the same sidewalk, the same man, the same peace he welcomed. The car hits the bar. The bartender's last act is the bottle flip — still perfect, still in the air, still spinning. The top-shelf glass hits Pepe with the accuracy of a man who never poured amateur. Then: the detective gets up. Fedora. Walks away. The case was solved. The loud sounds are gone. The mumbles cease. And the record stops — not because the music ended, but because the listener left the room. "Let's Twist Again" closes as the album's final refrain: the groove is still there. The groove is always still there. You just have to put the record back on. This track is Volume Two's Scene 014 — the hidden finale, the ending that was always coming, the thing at the end of the road you almost didn't take.
