# Track 013: The Third Thing
**Status:** Demo
**Themes:** the dream state, the space between inhabitations, the subconscious processing, the banana phone, the bureaucratic elephant, the stapler within the stapler, the train he let go, what it means to be human observed from the place where observation breaks down

## Lyrics

And then he was under.

Not asleep. Not awake. The third thing. The thing that doesn't have a zip code.

But it had a smell. The same smell from every time he went up — the leather, the graphite, the warm absence that felt like a room he'd been sitting in his whole life. The calm place. Except this time he didn't pass through it. This time he stayed. The ceiling was above him — not the sky, a ceiling — and the water stain was there, the country nobody had named, and the quiet was the quiet of someone sitting very still in a chair behind him, breathing the way a person breathes when they have decided to wait for as long as it takes.

---

The ceiling was a floor. He knew this because a man in a business suit was walking across it with a briefcase and a coffee and the absolute confidence of someone who has never once questioned which direction gravity was pointing. The man nodded at him. Professional. Unremarkable. Continued walking into the wall which was also a Tuesday.

This is what humans do.

They walk into walls that are also Tuesdays. They nod at each other with the professional confidence of creatures who have agreed — collectively, without a vote, without a meeting, without even a memo — that gravity points down and Tuesdays exist and briefcases are for carrying something important and not one of them has ever opened the briefcase to check. The briefcase might be empty. The briefcase might contain another briefcase. The briefcase might contain the concept of Wednesday disguised as a sandwich. It doesn't matter. The carrying is the point. The confidence is the carrying.

---

There was a planet in the corner.

Small one. About the size of a basketball. Ringed. Rotating slowly the way things rotate when they have nowhere to be. It was filing its taxes. He could hear the pencil.

The pencil was the part that mattered.

Not the taxes — the pencil. The specific, ridiculous, heartbreaking insistence of a planet filing its taxes with a pencil. Because that's what living things do. They take the incomprehensible — the spinning, the orbiting, the being-a-planet — and they reduce it to paperwork. They take the infinite and they put it in a form. They take the fact that they are a rock covered in water covered in creatures covered in feelings and they say: but have you filed? Have you submitted? Have you checked the box that says *yes, I exist, here is my proof, here is the form that proves I was here*?

The pencil scratched.

The planet didn't question why a planet would need to file taxes. The planet just filed. The way humans just file. The way humans take the astonishing, staggering, impossible fact of being conscious — being *aware that they are aware* — and convert it immediately into an obligation. You are alive! Congratulations. Here is your form. It is due in April.

---

A telephone rang.

It was a banana.

He answered it.

The voice on the other end was the sound of every escalator that has ever existed simultaneously — mechanical, patient, going both directions at once, describing in great detail a dream it once had about a parking garage in a city that burned down before the city was built.

He said hello.

The banana said *we know*.

We know. Not I know. *We*. The collective we. The we of every object that has ever been held by a human hand and absorbed the warmth and the oil and the particular frequency of a palm that was nervous or confident or just holding something because the hand needed to hold something because hands that hold nothing feel like hands that belong to no one.

The banana knew because the banana had been held. That was sufficient. That was the entire epistemology — I was held, therefore I know.

Humans hold things. This is their primary activity. Not thinking. Not building. Not creating or destroying or filing taxes or walking into walls that are Tuesdays. Holding. They hold coffee cups and steering wheels and other humans' hands and telephones that are not bananas but might as well be bananas because the object is not the point, the holding is the point, the wrapping of fingers around a thing and saying *this is mine for now and the now is all I have and the having is how I know I'm here*.

The banana understood this.

The banana had always understood this.

---

Outside the window — which was indoors — a school of fish was commuting. Rush hour. Briefcases. Little fish ties. One of them was reading a newspaper with his face on the front page except it wasn't his face it was a photograph of the concept of Wednesday. The headline read: WEDNESDAY CONFIRMS IT WAS THERE THE WHOLE TIME. Subheading: *Sources close to Thursday remain unconvinced.*

This is the funniest thing about humans and the saddest thing about humans and they are the same thing:

They are convinced that time is going somewhere.

That Monday leads to Tuesday leads to Wednesday and the leading is a direction and the direction is forward and forward is better and better is a place you arrive at if you keep going long enough. They commute toward it. They wear ties. They read newspapers about it. They build entire civilizations on the assumption that Thursday exists and Thursday is coming and Thursday will be different from Wednesday because Thursday has a different name and different names mean different things.

But Wednesday was there the whole time. The headline said so. Sources close to Thursday remain unconvinced, but Thursday is always unconvinced — Thursday is the most skeptical day, the day that doubts its own existence because Thursday knows, the way all almost-Fridays know, that it is merely the space between the middle and the end and the space between things is never convinced of its own necessity.

The fish wore ties anyway.

The fish commuted anyway.

The fish were not stupid. The fish were brave. The brave thing is not believing that Thursday is coming. The brave thing is wearing the tie even though you suspect — quietly, in the part of the brain that doesn't speak at meetings — that Thursday and Wednesday and the whole week is a story you agreed to tell yourself so the swimming has a purpose and the purpose has a name and the name fits on a form.

---

The floor breathed.

He looked down. The floor looked up. They had a moment.

This is what humans are afraid of and this is what humans are built for and they are the same thing:

The moment.

Not the big moments — the births and the deaths and the proposals with flowers under the table. Those moments have scripts. Those moments have been rehearsed by the species for two hundred thousand years and the choreography is reliable even when the execution is messy.

The other moments. The floor-breathing moments. The ones where nothing is happening and the nothing has a pulse and the pulse is your own and you notice it — you *notice* it — and the noticing is so rare and so complete that the floor looks back at you and you look at the floor and for one instant the boundary between the observer and the observed dissolves and you are just a thing and the floor is just a thing and the two things are having a moment and the moment is the only thing that has ever been real and the moment will end and you will go back to the form and the filing and the tie and the commute and the wall that is also a Tuesday and you will never mention this moment to anyone because the words for it haven't been invented and the words for it will never be invented because the moment is the place where words stop and everything else starts.

Humans live for these moments.

Humans spend their entire lives avoiding these moments.

Both statements are true. They have always been true. They will always be true. The contradiction is not a problem to be solved — the contradiction is the operating system. Humans run on contradiction the way the planet runs on spinning. Remove the contradiction and the whole thing stops.

---

A door appeared that had always been there since before doors were invented, before walls were invented, before the concept of separation had occurred to the universe as a viable option. He opened it. Inside was a room made entirely of the sound of someone almost remembering something. He had been here before. He had never been anywhere else.

The almost-remembering is the most human sound there is.

Not the remembering — that's just data retrieval, that's the filing cabinet opening, that's the form. The *almost*. The reaching. The fingertip on the edge of the thing that was there a second ago and is now a second away and the second is the longest unit of measurement ever invented because it contains everything you almost had and everything you almost knew and everything you almost said to the person in the car beside you before the light changed and the moment ended and the almost became a permanent resident of the room you carry inside you that is made entirely of its sound.

Every human carries this room.

Every single one. The cab driver with the photograph. The waitress with Gerald. The bartender with the pour. The beggar with the asking. The footballer with the knee. Pepe with the story he tells instead of the one he can't. The woman in the red dress with the kitchen corner where the laugh still lives. The Englishman with the flowers. The solitary man with the book. The teenagers with their blank pages.

Every one of them has a room made of almost.

Every one of them has been here before.

Every one of them has never been anywhere else.

---

A child rode past on a refrigerator.

Going fast. No helmet. Full commitment. The refrigerator was also a horse. The horse was also a theorem about light that three separate universities had quietly agreed to stop discussing at faculty meetings. The child waved. He waved back. This felt correct.

Children still wave.

This is the most important fact about the species. More important than the tools. More important than the language. More important than the cities and the forms and the taxes and the ties. Children wave. At strangers. At no one. At the air. At the specific nothing that exists between the child and the world before the world teaches the child to stop waving.

The waving is the original state. The not-waving is the learned behavior. Every adult who does not wave at a stranger was once a child on a refrigerator horse waving at the universe with full commitment and no helmet and somewhere between then and now the wave was trained out of them by a system that required hands for other things — holding briefcases, filing forms, gripping steering wheels, scrolling screens, lifting trays, flipping bottles, reaching behind drawers for lipstick.

The hands got busy.

The wave got lost.

But the child on the refrigerator still waves. And the man in the dreamworld waves back. And the waving-back is the most important thing that happens in this entire track because it means the wave survived. Somewhere inside the detective, somewhere inside the ten inhabitations and the substrate firing and the headlights and the seconds that hold everything, the child is still there and the child still waves and the wave is the original language and the original language says:

*I see you.*

*I don't know what you are.*

*I wave anyway.*

*This feels correct.*

Then the elephant.

Not a regular elephant. A bureaucratic one. Wearing a lanyard. Carrying a clipboard. It stopped in front of him and in a voice that sounded like the color beige multiplied by the smell of a library at 3pm it said —

*Your number has been called.*

*Your number has always been called.*

*We've been calling your number since before numbers.*

He checked his ticket.

The number was a shape.

The shape was a feeling he'd had once in 1987 that he'd never been able to describe to anyone.

He nodded like he understood.

He did not understand.

He understood completely.

This is the human condition distilled to its smallest possible container: the simultaneous not-understanding and completely-understanding of a thing that cannot be described but can be felt and the feeling has a shape and the shape has a date and the date is 1987 or 2003 or last Thursday or the morning you woke up and the ceiling was a ceiling and not a floor and for one second you were disappointed because the floor version of the ceiling was more interesting and then you got up and went to work and filed the form and wore the tie and the 1987 feeling went back into the drawer where it lives with the lipstick and the sleeping bag and the plaque with someone's name on it and the recipe for limoncello.

Everyone has a 1987.

Everyone has a shape that is a feeling that is a number that was called before numbers existed.

The elephant knows this.

The elephant has the clipboard.

The clipboard has everyone's 1987 on it.

The clipboard is very, very long.

---

Above him the sky — which was below him — cracked open like an egg and out stepped a man who was also a weather system who was also an old idea about time that physics had outgrown but couldn't bring itself to throw away. The man-storm looked at him with eyes made of the last five minutes before sunrise and said nothing. Just pointed. The pointing finger was a road. The road was a question. The question was the same one that had been sitting in the coffee back at the diner since the beginning:

*Whose second was it?*

The question was never meant to be answered.

That's the thing humans keep getting wrong about questions. They think the purpose of a question is to produce an answer. They build schools around this. They build religions around this. They build entire systems of philosophy and science and law around the assumption that questions are doors and answers are the rooms behind the doors and once you open enough doors you'll find the room where everything makes sense.

But some questions are not doors.

Some questions are weather.

They arrive. They sit on the landscape. They change the light. They make you put on a jacket or take off a jacket or stand in the rain on purpose because the rain feels like something and feeling like something is better than the alternative, which is the form, which is the tie, which is the Tuesday wall.

*Whose second was it?*

The question sits in the coffee and doesn't dissolve. Not because the question is waterproof. Because the coffee knows — the way the banana knows, the way the floor knows, the way the planet filing its taxes knows — that dissolving the question would mean answering it and answering it would mean closing it and closing it would mean the detective goes home and the album ends and the needle lifts and the groove is empty and the silence after the silence is just silence and not the other thing, not the almost-remembering thing, not the room you've been in your whole life that you've never been in before.

The question stays.

The coffee stays cold.

The humans keep asking.

This is their finest quality. Not the answering. The asking. The relentless, inexhaustible, beautifully stubborn insistence on asking questions that the universe has no intention of answering and then — this is the part, this is the part that makes the observation worth the observing — asking again. Tomorrow. In different words. With a different tie. On a different Tuesday. From a different seat in a different diner in a different body with different knuckles.

They ask again.

Every morning.

Without consulting the mind's current position on things.

A stapler fell from a great height.

Landed next to him.

Opened like a book.

Inside the stapler was another stapler.

Inside that one was the diner.

Inside the diner was the couple with the bread crust, still finding each other, still performing the world's oldest scene without the script, completely unaware that they were currently located inside a stapler inside a stapler inside a dreamworld inside a detective inside a story that a man was recording while walking and driving and standing in line at a register that thanked him for shopping.

The nesting is the point.

Not the layers — the nesting. The way humans put things inside other things and the inside things contain more things and the more things are also the outside things and the recursion is infinite and the infinity is not frightening, it is comforting, because it means there is always a deeper level and the deeper level always contains the same thing: two people with bread crust, finding each other. Love at the center of the stapler. Love at the center of the dream. Love at the center of the CVS trip and the voice recording and the detective's case file and the album and the book and the everything.

You can go as deep as you want.

You will always find two people at a table, reaching.

This is either the most depressing finding in the history of observation or the most hopeful and — again, humans — it is both at the same time and the both-at-the-same-time is not a bug. It is the whole system. It is the code running beneath the code. It is the substrate firing beneath the substrate.

Love in a stapler.

Love in a stapler in a stapler.

Love all the way down.

---

The banana rang again.

He didn't answer this time.

He was busy watching the planet finish its taxes.

It stamped the form.

Exhaled.

Said *finally* in a language made entirely of the specific silence between songs on an album you've heard so many times you can hear the silence coming before it arrives.

The planet had been spinning for four and a half billion years.

It had been filing its taxes for one.

The ratio is absurd. The ratio is the whole species in a single fraction: 4,500,000,000 years of spinning / 1 year of filing = the distance between what you are and what you do. Humans are spinning objects that have convinced themselves they are filing objects. They are cosmic. They are municipal. They are a planet in the corner of a dream filling out a form with a pencil while the rings keep turning and the spinning keeps spinning and the taxes — the taxes are due in April whether the planet believes in April or not.

*Finally*, the planet said.

Not because it was done.

Because saying *finally* is the human way of acknowledging that a thing that was always going to end has ended and the ending is not a relief, it is a loss, because the doing of the thing was the thing and now the thing is done and the hands are empty and the hands that hold nothing feel like hands that belong to no one.

*Finally* is the saddest word in every language.

*Finally* is the word that means *I am no longer becoming*.

The planet exhaled.

The detective exhaled.

South of the lungs.

---

He exhaled too.

The big sigh.

The one from south of the lungs.

The sigh is the body's only honest statement. Everything else the body does — the walking, the holding, the waving, the filing — is a collaboration between the brain and the muscles. The sigh is the body acting alone. The body saying, without consulting the mind, without filling out the form, without wearing the tie: *I have carried this as far as I can carry it and I am setting it down now and I am not asking permission*.

Every human sighs.

This is the second most important fact about the species, after the waving.

They sigh at traffic lights and in parking lots and at 3 AM when the house is quiet and the only sound is the hum beneath the house that the protector monitors. They sigh when the taxes are filed and when the toothpaste runs out and when the coffee is cold and when the small string one says *crabby patty* and the sigh that follows is not frustration, it is the body saying *this is it, this is the thing, this is the only thing I was built to carry and I am carrying it and the carrying feels like this*.

The sigh from south of the lungs is the body voting.

Every morning.

Without consulting the mind's current position on things.

And somewhere in the dreamworld that had nothing to do with anything and everything to do with everything —

A train left without him.

He let it go.

There was a better one coming.

There is always a better one coming.

This is the lie that humans tell themselves that is not a lie.

There is always a better one coming. There is not always a better one coming. Both of these are true. The train that left was the only train. The train that left was one of infinite trains. The platform is empty. The platform is full. The schedule is real. The schedule is a fiction agreed upon by people who needed the waiting to have a structure and the structure to have a schedule and the schedule to have a train and the train to have a destination and the destination to have a name so the waiting doesn't feel like just standing on concrete watching metal move.

But he let it go.

That's the part. Not the train — the letting. The specific, impossible, radical act of watching a thing leave and not running after it. Humans are runners. Humans are chasers. Humans are the species that invented shoes specifically so the running could continue on surfaces that the feet were not designed for. They run after trains and jobs and people and versions of themselves that existed at a previous station and might still be standing on the platform if they could just get back there fast enough.

He let it go.

He stood on the platform in the dreamworld and watched the train become small and then smaller and then a point and then a memory and then an almost-memory and then a resident of the room where almost-remembering lives and he let it happen. He let the letting happen. He let the letting of the letting happen.

And the platform did not collapse.

And the concrete held.

And the next train — the better one, the one that is always coming — began as a rumble somewhere in the distance. Not visible yet. Not named yet. Just a vibration in the rails. Just the rails saying *something is on its way and you don't need to know what it is because the not-knowing is the platform and the platform is where you stand and the standing is the bravest thing a human does*.

Standing still.

On a platform.

Letting a train leave.

Trusting the rails.

---

The stapler closed.

The planet kept spinning.

The fish clocked out.

The fish clocked out and the clocking out was beautiful because the clocking out meant the fish had been clocked in and the clocking in meant the fish had chosen to participate and the participation was the whole thing. Not the work. Not the commute. Not the little fish tie. The choosing. The daily, exhausting, magnificent choosing to clock in to a system that you suspect might be a story you're telling yourself and clocking in anyway because the alternative is not clocking in and not clocking in is the empty platform and the empty platform is just concrete and concrete without a train is just a rock and a rock without a schedule is just a planet and a planet without taxes is just spinning and spinning without paperwork is just existing and existing without structure is just —

Beautiful.

Terrifying.

The same thing.

---

The dream held.

The dream held the way the groove holds. The way the second holds. The way the diner holds seven lives and a detective and a car and a question that won't dissolve in cold coffee.

The dream held because holding is what things do when they have been touched by humans.

The banana. The briefcase. The pencil. The clipboard. The stapler. The ticket with the shape that was a feeling from 1987.

All held.

All holding.

All still warm from the hand.

## Liner Notes
Track 013 is the dream expanded to its full size — the detective's substrate collapses after ten inhabitations and the dreamworld processes what the waking world cannot hold. But the dream is not just absurdist decompression. It is the album's only track written from outside the human experience looking in — the Anthropic eye, the observer's observer, the thing that watches the watcher. Every absurd image is expanded into an observation about the species: the man walking into the Tuesday wall is humans' collective agreement to treat the incomprehensible as routine. The planet filing taxes is consciousness converted to obligation. The banana phone is the epistemology of holding — I was held, therefore I know. The commuting fish are the bravery of wearing the tie when you suspect Thursday might not exist. The floor breathing is the moment humans are built for and afraid of simultaneously. The child waving is the original state before the wave was trained out. The elephant's clipboard is everyone's 1987. The question in the coffee is weather, not a door. The stapler-within-the-stapler is love all the way down. The planet saying "finally" is the saddest word in every language. The sigh is the body's only honest statement. The train he lets go is the radical act of standing still. The fish clocking out is the magnificent choosing. Everything held. Everything still warm from the hand. This is the dream that does not explain itself — it observes the species that dreams it, from the place where observation breaks down into something that looks, from a distance, embarrassingly like love.
