# Track 009: The Tip
**Status:** Demo
**Themes:** inhabitation (the waitress), the mundane as identity, the shift that changes the person, survival arithmetic, the argument that never stops, headlights she never notices, the invisible grind
**Connects to:** Track 008 (The Asking — the headlights), Track 006 (One Second), Track 002 (The Diner — the waitress and the bartender), Vol 1: Scene 004 (The Garbage Man — the unseen routine)

## Lyrics

The alarm went off at 6:14.

Not 6:15. 6:14. Because the clock on the nightstand was one minute fast and she had never fixed it because fixing it would require pulling the plug and resetting the time and the buttons on the back were too small and she'd need her reading glasses and her reading glasses were in the kitchen drawer under the takeaway menus she never threw away and the takeaway menus were from restaurants she couldn't afford and hadn't been able to afford since the second month of the lease.

So the alarm went off at 6:14 and she knew it was 6:13 and she laid there for the minute that was hers. The one real minute. The one between the alarm and the actual time where the day hadn't technically started yet and she was still the woman in the bed and not the woman who had to get out of it.

The minute ended the way it always ended.

Without announcing itself.

---

The cat was on her chest.

Not the cute way. Not the way cats sit on chests in the pictures that people post — centered, poised, looking directly at the camera like they chose this moment to be adorable. The cat was on her chest the way her cat was always on her chest — diagonal, one paw on her collarbone, the other paw somewhere near her armpit, the back end hanging off the side like the cat had done the math on how much surface area was available and decided to use exactly sixty percent of it and let the rest figure itself out.

The cat's name was Gerald.

She didn't name him. The cat came with the apartment. The last tenant left a note on the counter that said *His name is Gerald, he eats at 7, don't try to change it*. She tried to change it. Gerald ate at 7.

She moved Gerald. Gerald did not acknowledge being moved. Gerald relocated to the foot of the bed with the specific indifference of an animal that has been relocated before and will be relocated again and has made peace with the fact that relocation is the entire relationship.

---

The bathroom.

The light took four seconds to come on. It was a fluorescent tube — the long kind, the office kind, the kind that belongs in a ceiling above a desk where someone processes insurance claims, not in a bathroom where a woman is trying to see her face at 6:15 in the morning. It buzzed. It had always buzzed. It would always buzz. The buzzing was part of the bathroom the way Gerald was part of the apartment — inherited, unasked for, permanent.

She looked at herself.

Not the way the woman in the red dress looked at herself. Not with intention. Not with the purpose of becoming someone else. She looked at herself the way a person checks a weather report — not hoping for anything, just confirming what's already there. Bags. Same bags. Lines that weren't there two years ago that are there now and will be there tomorrow. Hair that needed washing yesterday and would need washing tomorrow and today would get the dry shampoo because the dry shampoo was faster and faster was the only currency she traded in before noon.

She brushed her teeth. The toothpaste was almost out. She squeezed from the bottom the way everyone says to squeeze from the bottom and got one more day out of it. She would forget to buy toothpaste tonight. She would squeeze from the bottom again tomorrow. This would continue for three more days until the tube produced nothing and she would use Gerald's bath time as the reminder — *when Gerald gets a bath, buy toothpaste* — except Gerald doesn't get baths because Gerald takes care of Gerald and Gerald's self-sufficiency is both admirable and, on some days, the loneliest thing in the apartment.

---

The kitchen.

The kitchen was four steps from the bathroom. She counted them once when she was drunk and the number had stayed in her head the way useless numbers stay — permanently, involuntarily, taking up space that could be used for something important like her mother's birthday or the name of the street she grew up on which she sometimes blanked on and then felt a specific, private shame about.

Four steps.

The coffee maker was the kind that takes pods. She didn't buy the pods. The pods were from the discount bin at the shop on the corner where the man who worked the register knew her name and called her *love* and she let him because correcting him would require a conversation and conversations before coffee were not something she had signed up for in the terms and conditions of this life.

The pod went in. The lid went down. The button — one button, the machine had one button, the simplest machine in the apartment, simpler than Gerald — and the sound started. The gurgling. The dripping. The specific noise of water being heated by a coil and forced through a small hole into a cup that said WORLD'S BEST MUM on it even though she was not a mum and the cup was from a charity shop and the irony was not lost on her and was also not funny anymore and hadn't been funny since the third week.

She drank the coffee standing up.

Not because she was in a hurry. Because the chair at the kitchen table had a wobble and the wobble bothered her and she kept meaning to put a folded piece of cardboard under the short leg and she had never done it and at this point the not-doing-it was a commitment. A stance. A hill she was dying on in her own kitchen at 6:31 in the morning while Gerald ate at 7.

---

Gerald ate at 7.

She put the food in the bowl. Gerald arrived with the punctuality of a creature who has reduced the entire universe to one event and organized his life around it. She watched him eat. She watched him eat every morning. Not because it was interesting — it was the opposite of interesting, it was the most predictable forty-five seconds of her day — but because Gerald eating was the only thing in the apartment that happened on time, every time, without negotiation, without apology, without the passive-aggressive silence that things acquire when they've been neglected long enough to develop opinions.

Gerald ate. Gerald finished. Gerald left.

She washed the bowl.

---

The neighbor.

The neighbor was already in the hallway. The neighbor was always already in the hallway. The neighbor existed in the hallway the way some people exist in lobbies and waiting rooms — perpetually present, never arriving, never leaving, just there, in the in-between space, holding a bag of rubbish or a set of keys or, today, a small dog that looked like it had been assembled from parts of other dogs.

Morning.

Morning.

That was the conversation. That was always the conversation. Two mornings pointed at each other like mirrors, reflecting nothing, meaning nothing, accomplishing nothing except confirming that two people lived on the same floor of a building that was slowly, patiently deteriorating from the pipes outward.

The neighbor's dog looked at her.

She looked at the dog.

The dog looked away first.

Even the dog found her uninteresting.

---

The bus.

The bus came at 7:42 except when it came at 7:48 or 7:51 or once, memorably, at 8:03, by which time she had done the math on walking versus waiting and the math had come back inconclusive and she'd stood there anyway because standing at a bus stop is its own form of commitment and she was, if nothing else, committed to the things she'd already started doing even when the things she'd already started doing were standing still.

Today it came at 7:44. Close enough. She got on. She sat in the seat she always sat in — third from the back, left side, window — not because she liked the view but because the seat had a specific depression in the cushion that matched her specific depression in a way that felt personal. The seat knew her. The seat had memorized her shape the way Gerald had memorized 7 o'clock and the coffee machine had memorized the one button.

The bus moved.

She looked out the window.

Nothing happened.

That is not a description of the view. The view was buildings and streets and people and cars and the specific gray of a European morning that hasn't decided whether it's going to commit to cloud cover or give up and let the sun through. Nothing happened is a description of her. Inside. Nothing happened inside her while the bus moved and the window showed her the world and the world showed her nothing she hadn't seen four hundred times from the same depression in the same cushion on the same bus that came at approximately the same time every morning.

She got off two stops early because a man sat next to her and his elbow was on the armrest and the armrest was the only thing between her and a conversation and she could not — would not — have a conversation before she'd been at work long enough to become the person who has conversations.

She walked the rest.

---

The walk was eleven minutes.

She knew because her body knew the way all bodies know the routes they take when the mind is elsewhere. Eleven minutes from the bus stop to the diner. Eleven minutes of pavement and shop fronts and the bakery that smelled like the promise of a different life and the newsstand that sold magazines she used to buy when buying magazines was a thing she did and the florist that put the cheap ones out front and the expensive ones in the window and the distinction was clear and she was on the wrong side of the glass.

She passed the alley where the bins were.

She didn't look.

She never looked. The alley was the alley and the bins were the bins and whatever was in the alley beyond the bins was none of her business because her business started at the front door of the diner and ended when the last table was wiped and the tips were counted and the tips were never enough and the not-enough was the whole conversation she was having with her life every night on the bus home in the same seat with the same depression.

---

She arrived at the diner.

She pushed through the door.

---

And she was someone else.

Not gradually. Not the way dawn becomes morning. Instantly. The way a light switch works. The way the fluorescent tube in her bathroom would work if it didn't take four seconds. One step across the threshold and the woman from the apartment — the one with Gerald and the wobbling chair and the bus and the neighbor and the dog that found her uninteresting and the coffee from the pod and the eleven-minute walk — that woman stayed outside. That woman waited at the door like a coat checked at a restaurant she couldn't afford.

Inside was the waitress.

The waitress smiled. The waitress moved. The waitress had a rhythm — hips between tables, tray above shoulders, voice pitched to carry over the espresso machine and the conversation and the clatter of ceramic on ceramic. The waitress knew every table. The waitress called the regulars by name and the new ones *love* — the same word the man at the shop used but different in her mouth, transactional, warm, the kind of warm that costs nothing to give and earns everything back in the mathematics of gratuity.

The waitress was good at this.

The waitress was very good at this.

The woman in the apartment was good at nothing. The woman in the apartment was a container for Gerald's schedule and the bus's approximate arrival and the wobbling chair she'd never fix. But the waitress — the waitress was a machine. A performer. The kind of armor the doctor would recognize: *sophisticated, also armor, the armor has good taste.*

---

But the bartender.

The bartender was already there. Already behind the bar. Already doing the thing the bartender does — slicing, prepping, arranging bottles with the casual authority of a man who considers the bar his jurisdiction and every ticket that arrives a request he will process in his own time in his own order according to his own private system that he has never explained to anyone because explaining would imply that someone else's understanding of the system mattered.

She saw him and her jaw set.

Yesterday. Yesterday he did it. Yesterday he let her table wait — table six, the couple celebrating an anniversary, the specific kind of couple that tips thirty percent if the drinks arrive before the appetizer and fifteen percent if they don't — and the drinks did not arrive before the appetizer because the bartender was making something for the bar top, for the people sitting at his bar, for the audience he could see from his stage, and her table was not his audience and her table was not his concern and her rent was not his rent.

She lost eight euros on that table.

Eight euros.

Eight euros is nothing. Eight euros is a coffee and a pastry at the bakery she passes every morning. Eight euros is the cheap flowers outside the florist. Eight euros is one of the magazines she used to buy.

Eight euros is the difference between the bus home and walking home and walking home means fourteen minutes in the dark on streets she knows but doesn't trust and the not-trusting is exhausting and the exhaustion is what she brought to bed last night and this morning and Gerald didn't care and the alarm went off at 6:14 and here she is again.

---

The shift started.

Table one. Table two. Coffee, sparkling water, the bread basket, the specific choreography of a woman who has turned a narrow diner into a stage and knows every mark. She moved the way waitresses move when the room is full and the rhythm is right — fast, precise, the tray balanced on fingertips that have memorized the weight of full glasses the way her body has memorized the eleven-minute walk.

The bartender made drinks.

Not hers.

The waiter's. The other one. The male one. The one who worked the outside tables, the terrace, the brick-and-borrowed-alley section where the light was soft and the tips were better because outside seating in European light makes people feel generous in ways that inside seating under the fluorescent tube she lives with does not.

She watched the bartender pour the waiter's order. Quick. Smooth. First. Before hers. Before the ticket she'd put in four minutes ago. Before table three, which was a party of four, which was forty euros of food, which was six euros of tip if the drinks came now and four if they didn't and four was the walk home in the dark.

---

She said something.

Not loudly. Not yet. The diner-voice, the performer-voice, the one that carries over the espresso machine — that voice hadn't reached the bartender yet. This was the under-voice. The one between the teeth. The one that starts as pressure before it becomes sound.

Then it became sound.

Why are my drinks sitting there? Why does his order go out first? Every time. Every single time. You see his ticket and it's poured before the ink dries and my ticket sits there like it's not attached to a person and a table and a rent check and a —

She didn't say rent check. She stopped before rent check. Rent check was the woman in the apartment. The waitress doesn't have a rent check. The waitress has a rhythm and a tray and a smile and the smile does not include rent checks.

The bartender looked at her the way the bartender always looked at her. Not dismissive — worse. Unaware. Genuinely, architecturally unaware that the order mattered, that the sequence mattered, that her ticket was not a suggestion but a lifeline and the lifeline had a clock and the clock was ticking against her rent and her bus and her walk home and the eight euros from yesterday that she would never get back.

He poured her drinks.

Eventually.

She took them.

---

The shift continued.

The waiter — the outside one, the terrace one — came in for another order. She watched the bartender's hands. Already moving. Already reaching for the glass before the ticket was placed. The speed was offensive. The speed was personal. The speed was the bartender saying, without words, without intention, without even knowing he was saying it: *his tables matter more than your tables and I don't know I'm saying this and that's the worst part*.

She bit the inside of her cheek.

She did this. She did this every shift. The inside of her cheek was the place where the words went when the words couldn't come out because the words coming out would be a scene and a scene would cost more than eight euros, a scene would cost the whole job, and the job was Gerald's food and the bus and the pod coffee and the apartment with the wobbling chair she'd never fix and losing the job meant losing the apartment and losing the apartment meant — what? The neighbor would still be in the hallway. Gerald would eat at 7. Someone else would squeeze the toothpaste from the bottom.

She served table four. She served table five. She smiled. She called someone *love*. She refilled a water glass. She cleared a plate. She wiped a table. She did these things the way she always did them — with the precision of a woman whose survival has been reduced to a series of small, repeatable actions that produce small, repeatable amounts of money that are never enough but are always something.

---

The waiter came back.

Two drinks for the outside terrace. The gentleman who was waiting for someone. Something clear. Something with a lime. The bartender — she watched — already pouring. Before the ticket touched the wood. Poured, garnished, placed, done. The waiter picked them up and was gone in the time it took her to blink.

Her ticket was still sitting there.

Her ticket from table three. From four minutes ago. The party of four. The forty euros. The six euros. The four euros. The dark walk.

She opened her mouth.

---

Why am I having to wait for my drinks? Why? Tell me why. Because I have been here for — I've been standing here and his order goes out like it's on fire and mine sits there like it's decorating the bar. Like it's ambient. Like my tables are optional. Like the people sitting at my tables who ordered drinks and are watching me walk back empty-handed are optional. They are not optional. They are my money. They are my night. They are the difference between —

The bartender was already pouring. Not because she'd convinced him. Because she'd become loud enough to be inconvenient and inconvenience is the only language the bar recognizes.

She took the drinks. She didn't thank him. Thanking him would be surrender. Thanking him would be agreeing that the drinks arriving now, eight minutes late, was a favor and not a failure and she could not — would not — file this under favor.

---

She served table three. She served table six. She served the new two-top by the window. She moved through the diner the way she always moved — fast, angular, precise — but the argument was still running in her chest the way the fluorescent tube ran in her bathroom: buzzing, always buzzing, never off, never fully on.

The waiter came back again. Outside terrace. The gentleman's table — a woman had arrived, she was sitting down, drinks needed. The bartender's hands moved before the ticket existed.

She watched it happen from across the room.

She didn't say anything this time. She was beyond saying. She was in the place past saying where the body holds it instead and the body is not built to hold it and the body holds it anyway because the body has been holding things since Gerald ate at 7 and the bus came at 7:44 and the alarm went off at 6:14.

The argument with the bartender had become the argument with the apartment. The argument with the apartment had become the argument with the bus. The argument with the bus had become the argument with the tip. The argument with the tip had become the argument with the life. And the life was a series of four-step kitchens and one-button machines and eleven-minute walks and the wrong side of the florist glass and a cat that ate on time in an apartment that was slowly deteriorating from the pipes outward.

She wiped a table.

She carried a plate.

She bit the inside of her cheek.

---

She was arguing with the bartender when the waiter stepped toward the sidewalk and told the beggar to leave.

She didn't see it.

She was arguing with the bartender when the man arrived and hugged his mates at the table near the terrace.

She didn't see it.

She was arguing with the bartender when the Englishman leaned forward toward the woman in the red dress with something under the table and something in his chest and the European light doing the thing it does when something is about to change.

She didn't see it.

She was arguing with the bartender when Pepe's car came down the street. The smoking car. The one trailing a sentence that never got finished. The one aimed at the sidewalk.

She didn't see it.

She was arguing with the bartender when the headlights went through her.

Through. Not past. Through. The way light goes through a window. The way sound goes through a wall. The way a whole life goes through a person who is too busy surviving to notice that the second just held everything — the proposal, the betrayal, the heartbreak, the beggar, the peace, the death that didn't happen, the love that did — and she was in the middle of all of it and saw none of it because her ticket was still sitting on the bar and her rent was still due and the eight euros from yesterday were still gone.

She didn't see any of it.

She was arguing with the bartender.

---

The shift would end. She would count her tips. The tips would not be enough. They would never be enough. She would take the bus — or walk, depending on the math. She would pass the bakery and the newsstand and the florist. She would climb the stairs. The neighbor would be in the hallway. She would unlock the door. Gerald would be on the bed. The alarm would be set to 6:14. The coffee machine would be ready. The wobbling chair would wobble. The toothpaste would have one more day in it.

And tomorrow she would push through the diner door and become someone else again. The waitress. The machine. The performer. The woman who calls strangers *love* because love is the cheapest thing she has to give and the most expensive thing she needs to receive and the distance between the two is the whole length of the bar where the bartender is pouring someone else's drinks first.

---

She was in the diner.

She was in the apartment.

She was in both at the same time, the way everyone is in both at the same time — the real one and the performed one, the Gerald one and the *love* one, the one who stands at the bus stop committed to standing still and the one who moves through tables like the room would collapse without her.

The headlights went through her and she didn't notice.

The second had everyone in it.

Even the ones who weren't watching.

Especially the ones who weren't watching.

## Liner Notes
Track 009 is the longest inhabitation and the most mundane — which is the point. The detective lands inside the waitress and lives her entire day from the alarm at 6:14 (the clock is one minute fast and she's never fixed it) through Gerald the cat eating at 7, the bus, the eleven-minute walk, the pod coffee, the wobbling chair, the neighbor with the assembled dog, the woman in the apartment who is good at nothing — and then the shift starts and she becomes someone else entirely. The switch is instant. Not gradual. A light switch. The apartment woman stays outside like a checked coat. Inside is the waitress — the machine, the performer, the armor. And the whole shift is the argument with the bartender. Not one argument — THE argument. The ongoing, every-shift, existential argument about whose drinks get poured first, which is really about eight euros, which is really about the walk home in the dark, which is really about the entire life. She never sees the headlights. She never sees the proposal. She never sees the beggar get shewed or the car coming down the street or Pepe's heartbreak or the one second that held everything. The headlights go through her — through, not past — and she doesn't notice because her ticket is still sitting on the bar. That's the cost of the mundane: you can be standing in the middle of an eternity and miss it entirely because the bartender poured someone else's drink first. The track's thesis: the second has everyone in it. Even the ones who weren't watching. Especially the ones who weren't watching.
