# Track 006: One Second
**Status:** Demo
**Themes:** the proposal, the diner cast assembling, simultaneity from inside, inhabitation complete, one second as eternity, the crane returns
**Connects to:** Track 005 (The Wrong Reflection — he ran in), Track 002 (The Diner — same seven characters, now from inside), Vol 1: Scene 001 (seven billion cameras)

---

## Lyrics

She wasn't there yet.

That was fine. That was better than fine. That was the mercy of two minutes alone with the question before the question had someone to land on.

The diner. Outside seating. The same brick. The same borrowed alley in a country that had no business being this beautiful on a Thursday. He sat. The flowers went under the table where flowers go when a man doesn't want to look like a man holding flowers even though every person in a three-block radius could see he was a man holding flowers.

---

The beggar was there first.

Standing at the edge of the sidewalk the way he always stood — not angry, not dramatic, just present in the specific way that makes people work very hard to not see him. He asked the gentleman for some food. The gentleman did not look up. The beggar continued. The beggar always continues. That is his whole job — to keep being there after the not-looking-up happens.

---

The waiter arrived. Quick. Professional. The kind of efficiency that has a smile stapled to it.

Something to drink, sir?

Yes. Whatever he ordered — it doesn't matter what, the drink was never the point, the drink was what you hold while you wait for the thing that is the point.

Are you expecting someone?

He looked at the waiter the way a man looks at another man when the answer to the question is the biggest answer he's ever carried in his chest.

I am. She should be here soon. I'm going to ask her to marry me.

He said it out loud. To a stranger. Because sometimes you have to hear the words come out of your own mouth before you believe they belong to you. The waiter's face did something — not surprise, not performance, just the brief honest light of a man who has served a thousand tables and still finds this one worth pausing for.

Congratulations, sir. And if that gentleman on the sidewalk bothers you, please let me know.

He agreed. He would have agreed to anything. He was agreeing to everything today.

---

The waiter walked to the bar. The bartender was fast — already moving before the ticket landed, the way bartenders move when the rhythm is right and the room is full and everything is flowing the way a diner flows when nobody's watching the clock. The drink came back. The waiter delivered it. The gentleman accepted it and thanked him.

---

She arrived.

His heart stopped.

Not the metaphor. The actual mechanical pause where the chest forgets its assignment and the blood just — waits. Holds. Suspends itself between the last beat and the next one like a needle between grooves.

His mind spun.

He pulled the chair out for her. He spoke — and the words came out in a different tongue, the one that lives underneath the English, the one the body keeps for the moments that are too big for borrowed language. Excited. Nervous. Both at once. The flowers still under the table. Not yet. Not yet.

---

The waiter returned before she was fully seated. Not to miss a beat. Her drink order. She was still arranging herself — the purse, the coat, the specific choreography of a woman arriving at a table where a man is looking at her like she is the answer to a question he hasn't asked yet — and the waiter was already moving because the waiter understood that timing is everything and the drink is never the point but the drink arriving on time is part of the architecture of the moment.

He retrieved her drink.

---

At the bar, the waitress was arguing with the bartender.

Why am I waiting? Why are my drinks sitting there while his go out first?

Because her whole night depended on it. Her tips. Her rent. Her ability to close out the shift and go home and not do the math that tells her she came up short again. The bartender didn't understand the weight. The bartender served the waiter so fast because the waiter's table had a man with flowers under it and that felt like priority and priority is a decision someone makes that someone else pays for.

---

The waiter returned to the table. Set down her drink. She thanked him.

Then the waiter stepped forward. Toward the sidewalk. Toward the beggar who had been there the whole time, who had been there before the gentleman, before the flowers, before the question in the chest.

*Get out of here. You can't do this here.*

One sentence. The warmth left the sidewalk like someone opened a window in January.

---

At that second — that exact second — the man arrived.

The gentleman from the ride-share. Grateful, alive, calling out names. His mates were sitting right there, right beside the Englishman and the woman, and the tribe assembled in the time it takes to say a name loud enough for the whole diner to hear it. Hugs. Noise. The energy that fills a room without shrinking it.

While the waitress argued with the bartender.

While the beggar disappeared from the sidewalk like a sentence someone erased.

While the man sat alone at his table, reading his book, needing nothing from the room and giving it nothing back except the quiet proof that a person can be full without being filled by anyone else.

---

Beautiful day.

Beautiful.

---

And then —

As quick as it happened. As if he never left. The moving picture of everything that is happening — from above, from outside, from out of the body, from outside of whose body, what is happening —

He was up again.

The crane. The pull. The diner shrinking below him like a music box someone opened and all the figures were turning at once — the waiter stepping toward the beggar, the waitress shouting at the bartender, the loud man hugging his mates, the solitary man turning a page, the woman reaching for her drink, the Englishman with the flowers still under the table and the question still in his chest and the heart still remembering how to beat again —

All of it.

At once.

One second.

One second that is an eternity.

---

Seven people in a frame. Seven lives crossing the same square footage of borrowed European brick at the same moment on the same afternoon and not one of them — not one — seeing all of it.

Except him.

Except the detective, who was all of them and none of them, who was the Englishman with the ring and the waiter with the smile and the beggar with the asking and the bartender with the speed and the waitress with the argument and the loud man with the entrance and the solitary man with the book —

Who was floating above it now, watching the second stretch into forever, watching the music box turn, watching seven billion films play on one screen at one table on one street in one second that held, somehow, everything.

And the floating was quiet. Not silent — quiet. The difference between a room with no sound and a room where someone is breathing steadily across from you. He could almost smell it — leather, graphite, something clinical underneath the beauty. Something that had been there every time he went up and he'd never thought to name it because naming it would mean admitting the up was a place and the place was familiar and familiar things have addresses.

One second.

One second.

The needle doesn't move.

The groove holds.

## Liner Notes
Track 006 is the album's centerpiece — the one-second eternity. Every character from Track 002's Simultaneous Seven reassembles, but this time the detective is inside the frame, not watching from outside. He IS the Englishman. He's sitting at the table with flowers under it and a question in his chest. She walks in. His heart stops. He speaks in a different tongue because the moment is too big for English. And then the diner happens around him — all of it, simultaneously: the waiter serves and then erases the beggar, the waitress fights the bartender for her livelihood, the Uber man arrives with his tribe, the solitary man reads. The same seven from Track 002, same positions, but now experienced from INSIDE one of the bodies instead of from the detective's observation post. And then — the snap. Back up. The crane returns. The out-of-body from Track 002 Section VII, but now it hits different because the detective just lived inside the frame. He was all of them. He's none of them. The one second stretches into the whole album's thesis: simultaneity isn't observation, it's inhabitation. You don't understand the seven films by watching them. You understand them by being the Englishman with the flowers, and in the same second being the beggar who gets erased, and in the same second being the waitress fighting for her rent money. One second. Eternity. The needle doesn't move.
