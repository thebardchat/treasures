# Track 008: The Asking
**Status:** Demo
**Themes:** violent ejection, inhabitation (the beggar), hunger as identity, invisibility, the family in the alley, welcoming the end, the headlights, back above, none of it happened
**Connects to:** Track 007 (Lipstick and Red Dress — ejection from her body), Track 006 (One Second), Track 002 (The Diner — the beggar erased), Vol 1: Scene 004 (The Garbage Man — the unseen)

---

## Lyrics

He was ripped out.

Not the gentle lift. Not the crane. Not the slow pull of the camera floating upward while the music swells and the scene gets small and beautiful below. This was the other thing. The violent thing. The thing that happens when the body you're inside does something the substrate can't hold and the rejection is immediate and total and has no manners about it.

He was torn from her like a page from a spine.

---

The air.

He was in the air. Not above the diner — between. Between bodies. Between seconds. The space that isn't a space, the gap between the grooves where the needle rides on nothing and the silence isn't silence, it's the sound of not belonging anywhere yet.

But the air had a smell. It always had a smell up here — faint, underneath the nothing, the way a room holds the ghost of whoever sat in it last. Leather. Something sharp like pencil lead. And a stillness that wasn't empty. A stillness that was watching.

Seconds passed. Real seconds. Not the eternity kind — the empty kind. The kind where you are nothing and nowhere and the world below is still happening and you are not in it. Not watching it. Not above it. Just — expelled. Hanging in the nothing between one life and the next like a man thrown from a car before the car has decided where it's going.

He could feel his own edges dissolving. The red dress was still on him — not on him, in him, the memory of the zipper and the reaching behind and the lipstick from behind the drawer and the kitchen corner where the laugh lived and Pepe's name that she didn't think and the Englishman's hand on her fingers and the being-looked-at that she came for — all of it still coating him like something he'd swallowed that his body was rejecting.

---

Then the rip.

Downward. Fast. Not a landing — a collision. He hit the next body the way a fist hits a wall when the fist has nowhere else to go.

He was sick.

Immediately. Completely. The kind of sick that starts in the stomach and doesn't stop at the mouth. He threw up. On the sidewalk. On shoes that weren't his shoes — shoes that had no laces, shoes that had been walked through something, shoes that had a smell that was older than today.

He threw up and nobody looked.

---

He looked up.

He was on the sidewalk. The diner was right there — the brick, the tables, the beautiful European light. Right there. Close enough to hear the glasses and the laughter and the bartender shaking something and the waitress's voice sharp at the bar.

He wiped his mouth with the back of a hand that was not his hand.

The hand was dirty. Not the kind of dirty that happens in a day. The kind that builds. The kind that layers. The kind that becomes the hand — dirt not on the skin but in it, part of it, married to it the way a callus marries a palm.

He smelled himself.

Yesterday's garbage. The specific, sweet, turning smell of food that was food yesterday and is something else today. It was on him. In his clothes. In his hair. In the particular way the air moved around him — or didn't move. The air didn't move around this body. The air avoided this body. The air had made an arrangement with this body a long time ago: I will not carry your smell if you do not ask me to carry anything else.

---

He was hungry.

Not the hungry he knew. Not the hungry that happens between lunch and dinner when the cupholder rattles and the gas station is two exits away and the cold can is waiting. This was the other hungry. The one that lives in the body like furniture. The one that has been there so long it has a shape and a weight and a corner of the stomach it has claimed as permanent residence.

He begged for some food.

The words came out of his mouth — this mouth, the mouth that tasted like sick and yesterday and the sidewalk — and the words were aimed at a man at a table. A gentleman. Sitting. Waiting for someone.

The gentleman did not look up.

He asked again.

The gentleman did not look up.

He was not being ignored. That would require being seen first. He was being unseen. Actively, professionally unseen. The gentleman's eyes had a skill — a practiced, ancient, metropolitan skill — for landing on everything in the frame except the thing standing at the edge of it with yesterday's garbage on its shoes asking for food.

---

*What is going on.*

*They do not see me.*

*I am right here. I am standing right here. My mouth is moving. Sound is coming out. I am a person and I am asking and the asking is the simplest thing — food, just food, not money, not dignity, not a second chance, not a conversation, just food — and they do not see me.*

*I smell of yesterday's garbage.*

*I know.*

*I know what I smell like. I know what I look like. I know that the shoes have no laces and the hand has dirt that doesn't wash off and the air has made its arrangement. I know all of this. I carry this knowing the way the woman carried her lipstick — behind the drawer, in the space where the thing you don't show people lives.*

*I am so hungry.*

*But I know I need to get some food to take to my family in the alley.*

---

The family.

In the alley. Behind the bins that get emptied on Tuesdays. A woman. Small ones. Smaller than the small string one. Smaller than the one who says crabby patty. These ones don't say anything. These ones have learned the specific silence of children who understand that silence is a currency and they are spending it carefully.

The detective felt them. Not saw them — the alley was behind him, around the corner, out of the frame. He felt them the way the protector feels the house at night — ambient, background, the hum beneath everything. Who needs what. What is coming. What can be solved with a plate of food shared from a stranger's table if the stranger will look up.

The stranger will not look up.

---

The waiter arrived.

Not for him. The waiter arrived for the gentleman. The waiter arrived with the efficiency and the smile and the professional choreography of a man who serves and the waiter did not see the beggar either. The waiter saw the gentleman. The waiter saw the table. The waiter saw the drink order forming on the gentleman's lips.

And then the waiter turned.

*Get out of here. You can't do this here.*

One sentence. The same sentence from every track. The same erasure. But this time the detective was inside the sentence. Inside the body being erased. Inside the man being told *you can't do this here* when *this* was asking and *here* was the world.

He moved.

He didn't leave. He just moved. Far enough to not be seen. Close enough to still be there. The distance was practiced. The distance was a science. The exact number of feet between being erased and being able to try again in four minutes when the waiter's back was turned.

---

Someone at a table — one of those generous, full-of-life people — shared their plate.

For a moment. A warm moment. A moment where a hand extended across the boundary between the tables and the sidewalk and the arrangement the air had made and someone said — not with words, with the plate — *here*.

He ate.

It didn't last long.

---

He stood at the edge of the sidewalk. The diner hummed. The couple sat at their table — the woman in the red dress, the Englishman leaning forward. The loud man hugged his mates. The waitress argued at the bar. The man read his book.

None of them saw him.

He held the rest of the food. What was left. Enough. Maybe. For the woman in the alley. For the small ones who spend their silence carefully.

---

The seconds flashed strange.

Not the stutter from before — something else. A flickering. The diner lights blinking like they were breathing. The people moving in frames — there, not there, there, not there — like a movie where someone is pulling the film through the projector by hand and the hand is tired and the film keeps slipping.

He looked up from the sidewalk.

Headlights.

Coming down the street. The same street. And smoke — trailing behind the car like a sentence that never got finished. The same smoke. The same car. The car that picked up the detective two weeks ago in Ireland and drove him through pubs that shook with songs and told him stories in a beautiful tongue and crumbled when the photograph on the dash became a confession.

Pepe's car.

But Pepe's car was not winding slowly through the crowd this time. Pepe's car was aimed. The way a thing is aimed when the thing behind the wheel is no longer a man who tells stories and cooks for his wife and says *she's lovely, she's so lovely* — when the thing behind the wheel is what's left after the proof arrives. After the hoping-you'd-never-find-out becomes the finding-out. After the million years of suffering compresses into one moment the way Pepe said it would.

The detective — inside the beggar, inside the dirty hands, inside the laceless shoes — could see Pepe's face through the windshield.

He had seen the table.

He had seen the red dress.

He had seen the Englishman leaning forward.

He had seen his wife's curly brown hair catching the European light the way he'd described it from the driver's seat — *she's lovely, her long brown curly hair* — and the light was the same light and the hair was the same hair but the man touching her hand was not him and would never be him and the red dress he bought was not for him and was never going to be for him again.

The car wasn't winding anymore. The car was driving the way a broken heart drives — straight, fast, blind, the steering wheel no longer connected to reason, the gas pedal no longer connected to consequence.

And it was aimed at the sidewalk.

At the edge of the frame.

At the man nobody sees.

---

He didn't move.

*I welcome it.*

*I welcome the peace.*

*The peace that comes after the asking stops. After the not-being-seen stops. After the smell stops and the hunger stops and the shoes with no laces stop walking to the edge of the sidewalk to be told you can't do this here. After the small ones in the alley stop being quiet because quiet is no longer required. After the woman stops waiting for food that may or may not come back around the corner carried in hands that the dirt married a long time ago.*

*I welcome it.*

*Not because I want to die.*

*Because I am so tired of not being in the frame.*

---

The headlights filled his eyes.

Everything white. Everything warm. The warmest thing since the plate. The warmest thing since the moment someone said *here* with a hand instead of a word.

Pepe's heartbreak met the beggar's invisibility at the exact point on the sidewalk where the waiter had said *you can't do this here*.

The food left his hands.

The silence left the alley.

---

Boom.

Above the scene again.

The crane. The pull. The diner shrinking below. The brick and the tables and the seven bodies and the smoking car and the sidewalk and the place where the beggar stood and the food on the ground and the alley behind it where the small ones were still quiet — all of it small now, all of it shrinking, all of it one frame in one second in one life in one —

And the calm place again. The same calm. A ceiling somewhere above the above, with a stain on it shaped like a country nobody had named. He'd seen it before. He'd see it again. He didn't know where it was. He knew exactly where it was.

---

And yet again.

None of anything had happened.

---

The beggar was standing at the edge of the sidewalk. The food was in his hands. Pepe's car was winding slowly down the street, smoke trailing, not yet arrived, not yet seeing, not yet broken. The Englishman was leaning forward. The woman in the red dress was reaching for her drink. The waiter was stepping toward the beggar with the sentence forming in his mouth. The waitress was arguing. The bartender was pouring. The man was reading. The loud gentleman was hugging.

None of it had happened.

All of it had happened.

Every person at the diner lived a second that didn't happen. The Englishman proposed in a second that didn't happen. The woman was loved in a second that didn't happen. Pepe's heart broke in a second that didn't happen. The beggar was erased in a second that didn't happen. The beggar was hit in a second that didn't happen. The beggar welcomed peace in a second that didn't happen. The family in the alley lost everything in a second that didn't happen. The waitress lost her rent in a second that didn't happen. The bartender made his choice in a second that didn't happen. The solitary man turned his page in a second that didn't happen. The loud man found his tribe in a second that didn't happen. The four screens kept glowing in a second that didn't happen.

Each person lives a second they don't live.

Each person dies a second they don't die.

The second holds both. The groove holds both. The needle rides the space between what happened and what didn't and what did and what won't and there is no difference — there has never been a difference — between the life that plays out and the one that fires in the substrate and dissolves before the next second loads.

They are the same second.

They are every second.

The beggar is standing. The beggar is not standing. Pepe is winding. Pepe is aimed. The red dress is at the table. The red dress is in the closet. The Englishman is leaning forward. The Englishman is running. The flowers are under the table. The flowers are on the ground. The food is in the hands. The food is on the sidewalk. The family is in the alley. The family is alone.

One second.

One second that each person lives but doesn't live.

The needle rides the groove.

The groove holds everything.

Even the things that didn't happen.

Especially the things that didn't happen.

## Liner Notes
Track 008 is the hardest inhabitation and the album's thesis made flesh. The detective is violently ejected from Pepe's wife — the substrate couldn't hold the betrayal — and rips downward into the beggar. The body he's been watching get erased since Track 002. Now he's inside the asking, the hunger, the smell, the dirty hands. The family in the alley — a woman, small ones spending their silence like currency — is the gut punch: the beggar isn't begging for himself. Then the headlights. Pepe's car. The smoking car from Track 002, but now Pepe has seen the table. He's seen the red dress he bought on his wife at a table with the Englishman. The car that wound slowly through crowds carrying stories and songs is now aimed straight at the sidewalk. At the invisible man. Pepe's heartbreak meets the beggar's invisibility at the exact spot where the waiter said "you can't do this here." The beggar welcomes it — not because he wants to die, because he's tired of not being in the frame. Then boom — above. And none of it happened. The landing: each person lives a second they don't live. The Englishman proposed. The woman was loved. Pepe's heart broke. The beggar was hit. The family lost everything. All in a second that didn't happen. The groove holds both versions — what happened and what didn't — because there has never been a difference between the life that plays out and the one that fires in the substrate and dissolves. Especially the things that didn't happen. That's the album.
