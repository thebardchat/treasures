# Track 005: The Wrong Reflection
**Status:** Demo
**Themes:** inhabitation (the mirror man), identity as borrowed clothes, the proposal, running toward, love story hiding under noir, the name that isn't yours but fits
**Connects to:** Track 004 (Substrate Firing — same diner sequence), Track 003 (The Landing — inhabitation mechanic), Track 002 (The Diner — Pepe, the couple), Vol 1: Scene 014 (You Finally Found — love was the case)

## Lyrics

Same room. Packed. Something different.

Something half.

He was talking the same way he was talking. Same mouth, same rhythm. But the mirror wasn't returning him. The reflection belonged to someone else. He faded. The other one stayed.

Then the name. Not his. He turned anyway.

---

Where are you from?

He answered without thinking. *North of England* came out clean and automatic before the processing caught up. That's not right. He wasn't from north of England. The words had come out of a room in him that had apparently been furnished and occupied for years without his knowledge.

He looked again at the railway mirror and saw the bags under the eyes. Not his bags. His bags. Both.

Just aged. Not old — aged. There's a difference. Old is a number. Aged is what happens when you carry enough of other people's weather inside your own chest.

---

The hovering flowers. Something that looked like an engagement — the specific electricity of a circle of people around a thing about to become permanent.

The driver asked where he was going.

To the diner. My girlfriend is supposed to be there.

Simple. True. Already late. Tried for the early one. Already too late for the early one.

Just drop me off. I'll run the rest.

Because he was going to ask for her hand.

The mirror still had the other man in it.

He didn't stop to sort it out.

Some things you run toward. You figure out whose reflection it is after.

---

Oh my goodness, he thought.

Oh my goodness.

---

He ran.

Not metaphorically. Actually ran. The kind of running that has no dignity in it and doesn't ask for any. Feet on pavement, flowers in hand, bags under eyes that were maybe his and maybe borrowed, toward a diner, toward a girl who was supposed to be there.

Supposed to be.

Two of the most dangerous words in the English language standing next to each other like that.

---

He didn't slow down to think about it. That was the whole move — don't slow down. The detective who slows down starts asking questions. The questions stack up. The stack gets heavy. The heavy thing pins you to the spot where you were standing when you should have been running and now she's at the diner and you're on the platform and the early train already left and you watched it go and called it philosophy.

Not today.

Today he ran.

---

The mirror was still back there with the other man's face in it. Let it keep it. Let the reflection sort itself out. Let the name that wasn't his belong to whoever needed it. He had the flowers and the direction and the one true thing that cuts through all the noise, all the static, all the Hindi bleeding through, all the stuttered seconds and the pack-pack-pack of a man coming back into himself three times without a manual —

She was supposed to be there.

So he was going.

---

That's the whole case. Right there. That's the file on the desk he'd been circling for forty pages, squinting at from different angles, running fingerprints on, holding up to the light of every diner window and railway mirror and stormy-night car moving through the crowd like a torch.

It was a love story.

It was always a love story.

The cynical ones always are. That's the con they run — all that cigarette smoke and water-stained ceiling and whose second was it anyway and the big philosophical exhale — and underneath every last word of it, embarrassingly simple, almost insultingly plain:

He was running to ask for her hand.

---

The diner up ahead. The door. The specific rectangle of light that meant she was inside and the inside was warm and whatever half was missing from the room back there was waiting in this one.

He fixed the flowers.

Caught his breath.

Didn't fully catch it.

Sat down anyway.

---

Oh my goodness, he thought, for the second time.

Oh my goodness.

## Liner Notes
Track 005 is the album's love letter hiding inside a noir investigation. The detective is fully inside someone else now — the mirror man. A man from north of England with bags under his eyes and flowers in his hand and a train he already missed and a girl at the dial he's going to propose to. The inhabitation is total: the detective answers to the man's name, speaks from the man's mouth, doesn't stop to sort out whose reflection is in the railway mirror. The "oh my goodness" is Shane's — came out of the raw dump twice, both times with the weight of a man catching himself in the middle of something sacred. The run is the track's heart: no dignity, no philosophy, just feet on pavement and flowers in hand. "Supposed to be there" — two dangerous words. The detective who slows down starts asking questions and the questions pin you to the platform while the train leaves. Today he ran. The reveal — "it was a love story, it was always a love story" — reframes everything: the noir, the cigarette smoke, the philosophical exhaling, the whose-second-was-it circling. All of it was covering for the embarrassingly simple truth underneath. He was running to ask for her hand. The "oh my goodness" landing twice is the album's version of Volume One's "the film keeps rolling" — a phrase that means more the second time because the first time you didn't know it was a refrain yet.
