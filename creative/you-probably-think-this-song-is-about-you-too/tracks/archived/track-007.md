# Track 007: Lipstick and Red Dress
**Status:** Demo
**Themes:** inhabitation (the woman), the other side of the love story, the red dress as evidence, sneaking as ritual, guilt that doesn't feel like guilt, the diner from inside the betrayal, Pepe's wife
**Connects to:** Track 006 (One Second — the couple at the table), Track 005 (The Wrong Reflection — the Englishman's proposal), Track 002 (The Diner — the couple, Pepe, the Simultaneous Seven), Vol 1: Scene 018 (Dr. Seen — the wound)

## Lyrics

She started with the lipstick.

Not the one in the drawer — the one behind the drawer. The one that lives in the space between the back panel and the wall where the wood doesn't quite meet the plaster because the dresser was assembled in a hurry seven years ago by a man who builds things with love but not with a level. She reached behind. Found it by feel. The tube was warm, which meant the heating vent was doing its job, which meant the house was alive around her even though the house didn't know what she was about to do.

Houses never know. They just hold things.

---

The mirror was in the bathroom. Not the good mirror — the good mirror was in the bedroom and the bedroom had a window that faced the street and the street had neighbors and the neighbors had eyes and the eyes had opinions and the opinions had a way of traveling from the street to the kitchen table of the woman three doors down who talked to the woman at the market who talked to everyone.

The bathroom mirror was smaller. Honest in the wrong ways. It showed too much chin and not enough shoulder and the light was the kind that forgives nothing — the fluorescent kind, the institutional kind, the kind that says *I will show you exactly who you are and you will deal with it*.

She dealt with it.

---

The lipstick went on the way lipstick goes on when you've done it a thousand times in front of a man who isn't watching and once — just once, the first time — in front of a mirror for a man who would never see you put it on.

That's the difference. The ritual changes when the audience changes. For the man at home — she thought of him as the shape of a man in a doorway asking if she needed anything from the shop — for that man, the lipstick was maintenance. Part of the daily architecture of being a woman who keeps herself together because keeping yourself together is what women do in this town, in this alley, in this particular arrangement of brick and expectation.

For the other one, the lipstick was a door she opened.

---

The dress was in the closet.

Not the front of the closet. The back. Behind the winter coat that hadn't been worn since March and would provide cover until October. Red. Not bright red — the kind of red that knows what it's doing. The kind that sits in a room and doesn't announce itself until someone looks directly at it and then it's all they see.

He bought it.

That fact sat in the closet with the dress like a receipt she couldn't throw away. He'd come home from a long route — the kind that left smoke on his clothes and stories in his mouth — and handed her a bag from a shop she didn't recognize. The bag was wrong for the neighborhood. Too heavy. Too deliberate. The kind of bag that says *I thought about you while I was somewhere else and this is the proof*.

She'd worn it once, for him, at the kitchen table while he cooked and told her she was the most beautiful thing in the room and she'd said *you're not looking at the food* and he'd said *the food is not wearing a red dress* and they'd laughed and the laugh had lived in the kitchen for weeks and she could still hear it if she stood in the right corner, which she avoided.

She put it on.

For someone else.

The zipper ran up the back and she reached behind herself the way women reach behind themselves — alone, practiced, the specific choreography of getting into something beautiful without help — and the dress closed around her like an agreement.

---

Hair.

Brown. Curly. The kind that takes forty-five minutes to look like it took no time at all. She didn't blow it out. She let it fall the way it falls when she's not trying, which is the way she tries the hardest. There's an irony in this. She knows it. The mirror knows it. The house doesn't know it because the house is still holding things and not asking questions.

She checked the time.

He would be gone by now. The route. The long one. The one that started before sunrise and ended after the light changed and the smoke from the exhaust was the last thing the street saw of him until tomorrow. She had the hours. She had always had the hours. The hours were the space the life had made for this and she stepped into them the way she stepped into the dress.

Practiced. Alone. Reaching behind herself.

---

The shoes were by the door.

Not the front door. The side door. The one that opened onto the alley where the bins were and the bins were emptied on Tuesdays and today was not Tuesday so the alley was quiet and the quiet was hers.

She didn't check the street this time. She'd stopped checking the street four months ago. Checking the street was for the first time, the second time, the fifth time. By now the street was just the street and the alley was just the alley and the side door was just a door that opened in a direction that didn't face the neighbors.

She locked the house.

The key turned and the house held everything she was leaving behind — the kitchen where the laugh still lived in the corner, the dresser with the gap behind the drawer, the closet with the space where the red dress had been, the bed that was made because she always made it, the bed that would be unmade when she came back and made it again.

---

The walk was twelve minutes.

She knew because she'd timed it. Not on purpose — her body had timed it the way bodies time the routes they take when the mind is busy with other things. Twelve minutes from the side door to the alley where the diner sat in the kind of European light that had no business being this soft, this specific, this willing to make everything look like a painting.

She passed a man reading a book at a table outside a café that wasn't the diner but was close enough to the diner that the man's presence felt like a preview. He didn't look up. She liked that. She liked being unseen for the twelve minutes. The twelve minutes belonged to the woman in the red dress and the woman in the red dress was not the woman married to the man with the smoking car. The woman in the red dress was someone else. Someone who walked through European alleys with her curls falling the way they fall when she's not trying and her lipstick from behind the drawer and her shoes from beside the side door.

Someone with no kitchen corner.

Someone with no receipt.

---

She saw the diner.

The brick. The outside seating. The small tables pressed close together because this was Europe and in Europe the tables are close and the conversation is everyone's and the privacy is a performance you agree to by sitting down.

She saw him before he saw her.

He was at the table. The Englishman. Sitting the way men sit when they are waiting for a woman they are not supposed to be waiting for — which looks exactly like the way men sit when they are waiting for a woman they are absolutely supposed to be waiting for. There is no difference. The body doesn't code for guilt. The body codes for anticipation and anticipation looks the same whether the thing you're anticipating is a birthday party or the end of the world.

He had something under the table. She couldn't tell what.

---

She paused.

Not because she was reconsidering. Because the twelve minutes were over and the next step was the step into the frame and the frame had other people in it and other people had eyes and the eyes had the same opinions as the neighbors' eyes and the opinions traveled the same way.

But the dress was already on. The lipstick was already on. The side door was already locked. The house was already holding the things she'd left behind.

She stepped into the frame.

---

A beggar at the edge of the sidewalk looked at her. Not the way men look — the other way. The way a person looks at another person when they have been looked through so many times that any pair of eyes willing to land on them, even for a second, feels like weather breaking. She didn't stop. She wanted to stop. She walked past him the way everyone walks past him and filed the want-to-stop somewhere she wouldn't look at until later, probably in the bath, probably with the water too hot, probably with her eyes closed.

The waiter was already moving.

He cut between the tables with the professional grace of a man who has turned a narrow alley into a stage and knows every mark. He arrived at the Englishman's table just as she approached. The timing felt choreographed. It wasn't. It was the diner breathing — everything happening at the moment it was supposed to happen, every body arriving at the position the second required.

The Englishman stood.

---

He looked at her the way a man looks at a woman in a red dress when the red dress is the only thing he has been thinking about for three hours and the woman inside it is the only thing he has been thinking about for longer than he can organize into a number.

She sat.

He pulled the chair. He spoke. The words were in a different tongue — his tongue, the one from north of England, the one that falls out of the mouth like stones skipping on water — and she understood him the way she always understood him, which was not through the words but through the weight of the words.

The waiter appeared.

Something to drink?

She ordered. She didn't hear herself order. Her mouth handled it. Her mouth had been handling things all day — the silence at home, the side door, the twelve-minute walk — and now her mouth was handling a drink order while her eyes were on the Englishman and his eyes were on her and the beggar was at the sidewalk and the waiter was writing and the tables were close and the brick was glowing and the whole diner was one machine with seven moving parts and she was the newest one to arrive and the only one wearing something bought by a man who was not at this table.

---

The waiter returned with her drink. Set it down. She thanked him.

The waiter stepped forward.

Toward the sidewalk. Toward the beggar.

*Get out of here. You can't do this here.*

She watched. The Englishman didn't watch. The Englishman was looking at her. She was the only movie playing on his screen and she knew this and the knowing was the thing she came for — not the drink, not the food, not the diner, not the European light — the knowing. The being-looked-at by someone who saw only her and would see only her for the duration of this meal, this hour, this borrowed second.

The beggar moved. Didn't leave. Just moved. The way he always moves. Far enough to not be seen, close enough to still be there.

---

At the bar, a waitress was arguing with the bartender.

She could hear it from the table — the sharp, specific frustration of a woman whose income depends on someone else's competence. The bartender had served the waiter's order fast — her drink, the Englishman's drink — and the waitress's table was still waiting and the waitress's table was her rent and her rent was late and the lateness had a shape and the shape was the bartender's back turning away from her tickets.

She listened.

She understood the waitress in a way the Englishman never would. Not because women understand women — that's too simple, too much of a brochure. Because she knew what it was to have your survival depend on the timing of someone else's hands. The bartender's hands. Her husband's hands on the steering wheel. The Englishman's hands — reaching across the table now, finding hers, his fingers landing on her fingers the way fingers land when the body already knows what the brain is still debating.

---

A man arrived. Loud. Grateful. Alive in the way that fills a room without asking permission. He was meeting friends — she could see the table, right there, close, the way all the tables were close. The hugs. The names called out. The tribe assembling.

She watched him the way she watched everyone today — from inside the red dress, from inside the lipstick, from inside the twelve minutes that had delivered her here. He was joy without complication. She envied that. She envied it the way you envy a thing you used to have, the way you envy the version of yourself that laughed in a kitchen while a man who loved you said the food is not wearing a red dress.

She blinked.

The Englishman squeezed her hand.

She squeezed back.

---

At a table near the bar, a man sat alone. Reading. A book held the way books are held by people who need nothing from the room — open, tilted, the pages catching the light the way pages catch light when they are the whole world and the world outside them is just the sound of a diner doing what diners do.

She envied him too.

The simplicity of it. The fullness of needing only a book and a table and the specific quiet of being complete without another person's eyes confirming it.

She was not that.

She had never been that.

She was the woman who needed to be seen, who needed the looking-at, who had walked twelve minutes through a European alley in a red dress bought by one man to sit across from another man because the looking-at was the thing that made the second hers.

---

The Englishman leaned forward.

He was going to say something. She could feel it the way you feel weather — not the rain, the pressure before the rain. The drop in something. The specific density of air that means something is about to change and the changing is already done and the announcement is just the world catching up to the physics.

She didn't know about the flowers.

She didn't know about the question in his chest.

She didn't know that in one second — the next second, the one assembling itself right now in the substrate of the diner, in the firing beneath the brick and the light and the seven bodies and their seven films — everything in the frame would happen at once and the crane would pull back and someone above it all would see the whole picture and the whole picture would include her, in the red dress, bought by Pepe, sitting across from a man who was about to ask for her hand while the man who already had it drove a smoking car somewhere on a road that led, eventually, to this alley, to this diner, to the exact table where his wife sat in the dress he bought her being looked at by someone else.

She didn't know any of that.

She just knew the lipstick was right. The dress was right. The twelve minutes were over.

And the Englishman was leaning forward.

And the waiter was shooing the beggar.

And the waitress was fighting for her rent.

And the man with the book wasn't looking up.

And the loud man was hugging his mates.

And somewhere — somewhere on a road she couldn't see from this table — a car was trailing smoke like a sentence that never got finished.

And the second was everyone's.

And the second was no one's.

And the red dress was Pepe's.

## Liner Notes
Track 007 is the album's darkest inhabitation — the detective lands in Pepe's wife. The woman from Track 002 with the brown curly hair and the green eyes and the red dress. The woman being fed at the table by the Englishman. The woman Pepe described with such tenderness from the driver's seat: "she's lovely, she's so lovely, her long brown curly hair, and the way she loves food." We follow her from the beginning — the lipstick behind the drawer, the dress from the back of the closet, the side door, the twelve-minute walk. Every detail is sneaking: the bathroom mirror instead of the bedroom mirror, the side door instead of the front, the shoes positioned for exit. The dress is the evidence and the weapon — red, bought by Pepe, worn for someone else. The reader doesn't know whose body they're in until the details accumulate: the brown curly hair, the red dress, and then the final line — "and the red dress was Pepe's." The diner cast assembles around her the same way it assembled in Track 002 and Track 006, but now from inside the betrayal. The beggar gets shewed. The waitress fights. The Uber man arrives. The solitary man reads. And the Englishman leans forward with flowers under the table and a question she doesn't know is coming. The last image — Pepe's car trailing smoke somewhere on a road she can't see — is the sentence that never got finished from Track 002. The detective is inside the one person in the diner who is breaking Pepe's heart. That's the cost of substrate empathy — you don't get to choose which body needs you. Sometimes you land in the wound.
