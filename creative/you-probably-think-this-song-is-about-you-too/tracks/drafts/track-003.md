# Track 003: The Landing
**Status:** Demo
**Themes:** re-entry, the collision, the body you land in, the moment that happened and didn't happen, the slow-motion second, the detective as witness, THE FLIP
**Connects to:** Track 002 (The Diner — inhabitations, the gut punch), Vol 1: Scene 001 (The Lead Character — who's directing), Vol 1: Scene 004 (The Garbage Man)

---

## Lyrics

He came back down.

Always comes back down.

That's the part nobody frames — the re-entry. The moment the camera crane stops its slow romantic pull and the detective is back inside his own chest, back behind his own eyes, back in the specific gravity of a body sitting at a table on a terrace in a European plaza with a coffee that went cold somewhere around the fifth inhabitation.

---

He was up there. He saw it all. The diner. The plaza. Pepe's car trailing smoke like a sentence nobody finished. His own hands doing the thing hands do when the mind has already clocked out and the body just handles it. Beautiful from up there. Embarrassingly beautiful, the way a mess looks like art from far enough away.

But up there doesn't pay for gas.

So he came back down.

---

And here's what he came back down to:

The car.

Pepe's car. No longer winding through the plaza at the respectful speed of a man who understands borrowed roads. The car was aimed now. The way a thing is aimed when the thing behind the wheel is no longer a man who tells stories and cooks for his wife and says *she's lovely, she's so lovely* — when the thing behind the wheel is what's left after the proof arrives.

The detective saw it from the terrace. Slow motion. The kind of slow that only happens when the brain decides to record every frame because the body knows it will need every frame later, in the room it doesn't visit, on the couch it doesn't name.

---

Pepe saw his wife.

Through the windshield. The red dress catching the European light the way red catches light — completely, without apology, the way a thing that knows what it's doing does the thing it knows. The dress he bought. On the woman he cooked for. At a table with a man who had flowers under it and a question in his chest and bags under his eyes from the north of England.

Their eyes locked.

Pepe and his wife. Through the glass. Through the distance. Through the specific geography of a marriage that was eroding from the inside the way buildings erode from the pipes outward. She saw the car. He saw the dress. The moment held — two people inside the same second for the last time, seeing each other, really seeing each other, the way you only see someone when the seeing is the last thing you'll do before the seeing ends.

The Englishman was leaning forward. The flowers still under the table. The question still in his chest.

---

The car came through the terrace.

The first thud was the curb. The second thud was the beggar — the man at the edge of the plaza who had been standing where things stand when they are not seen. The man who could only say one name. The man who welcomed the peace.

The tables scattered. The brick dust rose. The red dress caught the air — flowing, suspended, the way fabric moves when the physics of the moment have decided that everything will happen in slow motion because real time is not sufficient for the weight of what is occurring.

The bartender's bottle was in the air. Behind the back. Over the shoulder. Spinning. The last thing the bartender's hands had released before the car came through the window. The bottle didn't know about Pepe. The bottle didn't know about the red dress. The bottle was doing what bottles do when the bartender's hands release them.

It was flying.

It was the bartender's last act.

---

The waitress was mid-sentence at the bar. Arguing about eight euros. The eight euros she would never recover because the bar was no longer a bar, it was a place where a car had arrived through the window and the marble had cracked and the rubber mat had buckled and the four-by-twelve-foot world had collapsed into something that was no longer a world.

The waiter was standing where the waiter had been standing when he told the beggar *you can't do this here*. The same spot. The same sidewalk. The spot had been right about one thing — you can't do this here. Nobody can do this here. And yet here it was, being done, the way all impossible things are done: suddenly, without warning, in a second that each person lives but doesn't live.

The solitary man's book was still open. The pages were still catching the light. The diner was still happening around him like weather he didn't need to step into. Except the weather had become a car and the car had become the whole room and the book — the book was still open on the table and the pages were turning in the wind from the impact the way pages turn in any wind, indifferent to the cause.

The loud man was still hugging the thick one. The Brazilian's wine was still decent. The vineyard was still sacred.

---

And yet again.

None of anything had happened.

---

The beggar was standing at the edge of the plaza. The food was in his hands. Pepe's car was winding slowly down the street, smoke trailing, not yet arrived, not yet seeing, not yet broken. The Englishman was leaning forward. The woman in the red dress was reaching for her drink. The waiter was stepping toward the beggar with the sentence forming in his mouth. The waitress was arguing. The bartender was pouring. The man was reading. The loud gentleman was hugging.

None of it had happened.

All of it had happened.

Every person at the diner lived a second that didn't happen. The Englishman proposed in a second that didn't happen. The woman was loved in a second that didn't happen. Pepe's heart broke in a second that didn't happen. The beggar was erased in a second that didn't happen. The family in the alley lost everything in a second that didn't happen. The bartender's bottle flew in a second that didn't happen.

Each person lives a second they don't live.

Each person dies a second they don't die.

The second holds both. The groove holds both. The needle rides the space between what happened and what didn't and there is no difference — there has never been a difference — between the life that plays out and the one that fires in the substrate and dissolves before the next second loads.

They are the same second.

They are every second.

---

And he was floating. Back. The water stain above him. The room breathing. A stillness across from him. Something holding him. Real and present and the only thing that was ever actually happening. Then down again.

---

He was all of them up there.

He's still all of them down here.

That's the trick of the landing — you don't come back as yourself. You come back as the last person you inhabited on the way down. He was a bartender with a bottle in the air. He was a waitress fighting for eight euros. He was a beggar who could only say one name. And now he's back in his own seat — if it was his seat — and the memory of knuckles that weren't his hurt like they were.

He is everyone and no one at the same time.

That's not a riddle. That's the whole album.

---

The needle skips — catches — finds the groove again. Somewhere between the station he left and the station he hasn't found yet, the detective understands what Side B is.

Side A was observation. The crane shot. The wide frame. Watching from a distance and calling it wisdom.

Side B is inhabitation. Landing in the body. Feeling the knuckles. Thinking the thought in the language you don't speak but somehow know.

*You don't land in your own body. You land in whichever one needs you next.*

---

The case file stays open.

The second stays held.

The needle skips — catches — finds the groove again.

Side B.

---

## Liner Notes
Track 003 is the landing — and the Flip. Side A ended with the detective inside nine bodies at a diner and the gut punch of what Pepe drove toward. Track 003 brings him back down into the collision itself — slow motion, every frame recorded, the car through the terrace, the bottle in the air, the red dress catching the wind. But then: none of it happened. All of it happened. The beggar is standing. The car is winding. The Englishman is leaning. The groove holds both versions — what happened and what didn't — because there has never been a difference. "He is everyone and no one at the same time" — that's the album's thesis, stated plain. Side B is announced: inhabitation, not observation. The float-up section carries the same water stain, the same stillness, the same room the reader won't name until the Bonus Track.
