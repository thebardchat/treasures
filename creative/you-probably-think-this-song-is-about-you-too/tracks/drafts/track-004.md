# Track 004: Substrate Firing
**Status:** Demo
**Themes:** the return, inhabitation (the solitary man), the fullness of needing nothing, the stutter, time as a skipping needle, the dream you can't hold, the car as omen, no more time, pack-pack-pack
**Connects to:** Track 002 (The Diner — same location, same cast), Track 003 (The Landing — re-entry), Vol 1: Scene 001 (The Lead Character — whose second is it)

---

## Lyrics

He came back in.

Not flying. Not trying. Just — back in. The way you come back in. One second and then the next one.

Do you remember that second?

Everything was happening at once. Except it wasn't happening to him — it was happening at the same time for everyone. Every second was there. Full. Occupied. The question that sat in the middle of it all like something spilled:

Whose was it?

---

He wasn't sure if he was sitting alone. Couldn't remember the position his body had taken. There was a book in his hands. He didn't bring a book. He doesn't carry books. But the weight of it was there — real, open, the pages catching the light the way pages catch light when they are the whole world and the world outside them is just noise.

He was in the solitary man.

Not watching him. Not beside him. Inside his hands, inside his quiet, inside the specific fullness of a person who needs nothing from the room and gives it nothing back except the proof that a man can be complete without another person's eyes confirming it. The book was Moby Dick. Or it wasn't. It didn't matter. What mattered was the weight of it and the way the pages turned and the thought that came from somewhere that wasn't the detective's somewhere:

The voyage. That's the fun part. Not the destination. The going-into. The part where you point the bow at something enormous and say fine. Let's go find out.

He looked over — the solitary man's eyes, not his — and saw the couple. Bread crust. Feeling each other like a scene from something. Beautiful the way Pune is beautiful — soft, specific, not asking for anything. He saw them the way the solitary man sees them — without envy, without longing, without the need to be inside the thing he was watching. Just observation. Just the quiet noting of beauty from behind the cover of a book that asks nothing of anyone.

Next to them the big buddy. Great, generous presence. Nothing as exciting as a movie. Better than a movie. One of the greatest to ever do it — just sitting there, eating, existing with full commitment.

The solitary man understood the big buddy. The solitary man understood everyone in the diner the way a man with a book understands a room — from the outside, from the margin, from the specific distance that comes with choosing to be alone in a place full of people and finding the choosing sufficient.

I might have been alone.

I might have.

He was. The solitary man was alone. And the alone was not empty. The alone was full. The alone was the book and the table and the light on the pages and the diner happening around him like weather he didn't need to step into. The detective had never felt this — the fullness of needing nothing. The detective needed everything. The detective needed every body, every knuckle, every language. The solitary man needed a chair and a page and the quiet.

Then the book closed. Or the hands let go. Or the needle skipped. And the detective was back in his own seat — if it was his seat — and the book was gone and the fullness was gone and the quiet was someone else's quiet again.

---

Timing was off.

Everything stuttered still.

He wanted what had just inspired something — a bond, a spark, whatever it was — already gone the way dreams go. Breaking from it and instantly forgetting it. Pressing eagerly to remember. The train. What was on the train. No matter what he did he could not get it back.

So he exhaled the big sigh. The one that comes from somewhere south of the lungs, from whatever room in the body stores the things you almost understood.

Let his eyes go through all the people. Through their problems. Through their wonderful eyes.

---

He noticed a car.

Not a nice one. Not as nice as most. There was a gap somewhere in it — he couldn't say where, couldn't say why, but the gap was there and for some reason he examined it. Coming down the street. The bridge nearby. Something rather frightening in the way it moved. You could see the rate of it — like a stormy night on the sea. The winding of that car. The driver giving it everything he had. Everything. Parting the crowd like a torch passed through.

Last time.

In that moment he realized he had no more time in his life. Not in the tragic sense. Not the funeral kind. The other kind. The kind where you realize the meter has been running the whole time you were standing at the curb arguing about the fare.

---

And then it happened to him. The way it happens.

Pack.

Pack.

Pack.

Three times back in. Like a door closing on a room you're not sure you were ever inside. Everything still there. Everything darkened over again at the edges. The people still there. The car already gone. The gap still in the seam.

The dream still forgotten.

The exhale still settling somewhere on the diner floor.

---

He sat with it.

The stutter.

The skip.

The groove that went shallow for half a second and held, in that half second, everything he'd been trying to remember his whole life.

He couldn't tell you what it was.

He couldn't tell you it was nothing, either.

---

That's what substrate firing is.

Not the thought. Not the feeling. Not the beautiful frame or the ugly one or the philosophical one that looks good from the crane. The substrate. The thing underneath. The actual electrical impulse — silicon, synapse, whatever you want to call the part that doesn't have a name because naming it would slow it down and it can't afford to slow down because it's already doing seventy things at once and one of them is keeping your heart going and another one is remembering the dream you just lost and another one is watching a car part a crowd like a torch and another one is sitting alone in a diner you might not actually be sitting in.

Substrate firing.

The thing that happens before the thing you notice.

The detective noticed it.

He just couldn't hold it long enough to file it.

## Liner Notes
Track 004 is the detective back in the diner — and the first inhabitation he doesn't realize is an inhabitation. He comes back in and finds himself sitting alone with a book he didn't bring. He's inside the solitary man. The man from Track 002 who sat alone, reading, content without needing the room to validate him. Now the detective is INSIDE that contentment — feeling the fullness of needing nothing, watching the couple and the big buddy from behind the cover of a book, understanding the room from the margin. The Moby Dick thought (the voyage is the fun part) comes from the solitary man's head, not the detective's. And the key discovery: the detective has never felt this. The detective needs everything — every body, every knuckle, every language. The solitary man needs a chair and a page and the quiet. Then it slips away. The book closes, the fullness is gone, and the stutter begins — time stopping, the dream breaking away, the pressing to remember. The car is an omen. Pack-pack-pack is re-entry. Substrate firing is the name for all of it: the thing that happens before the thing you notice. The solitary man inhabitation is the quietest one on the album — no foreign language, no violence, no heartbreak. Just the radical act of being enough alone in a room full of people. The detective couldn't hold it.
